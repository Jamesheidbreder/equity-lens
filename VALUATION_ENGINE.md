# The Valuation Engine

It answers one question: **what is one share of this company actually
worth?** Real data, open math, no black box.

**The flow:** SEC filings + market prices + Fed data → three lenses →
one blended target → rating → public scorecard.

## Inputs

Audited financial statements from the SEC. Live market prices.
Interest rates and economic data from the Federal Reserve. All free,
all public.

## Three independent lenses

1. **Discounted cash flow** — the cash the business will generate,
   translated into today's dollars. *What are the future profits worth
   now?*
2. **Peer comparison** — the price similar companies command, applied
   here. *What would it cost priced like its competitors?*
3. **Own history** — the price this stock has usually commanded,
   applied to today's earnings. *What would it cost priced like
   itself?*

## From lenses to a rating

- **Blend.** The three answers combine into one target price.
- **Rate.** Target more than 10% above the market price = BUY. More
  than 10% below = SELL. Between = HOLD.
- **Macro.** The economy leans on the inputs in small, capped ways —
  higher rates lower every value; costlier aluminum squeezes a soda
  maker.

## Three rules that keep it honest

- **Sector playbooks.** A bank is not valued like a phone maker.
  Each company's methods are declared by sector, in writing — never
  silently auto-detected.
- **Disclosed judgment.** The machine prices only what has happened.
  Unproven stories (a new product, an AI bet) enter only through a
  dated, written adjustment — and both numbers are always shown.
- **Conviction from disagreement.** When the lenses agree, confidence
  is high. When they disagree wildly, the value is genuinely
  uncertain, and the report says so instead of pretending precision.

Every rating and target is logged with its date and can never be
quietly rewritten.
